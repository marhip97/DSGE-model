                                        Abstract

This paper documents GEORG, "Ganske Enkel Optimal ReGel", a policy rule designed to
replicate the loss function based policy rate path in Norges Bank’s main macro model,
NEMO. The rule is forward looking and uses a selection of macroeconomic indicators of
inflation and output developments. Coefficients are estimated by impulse response matching
(IRF matching), ie the parameters are set so that the model dynamics of using GEORG in
NEMO closely match those of the standard loss function based optimal policy. We outline
how the rule can be used to compute a model based policy rate path and how to decompose
model based path changes into intuitive forecast revisions.
1        Introduction
Norges Bank’s monetary policy analysis relies on the structural DSGE model, NEMO, in which
the policy rate is determined by minimizing a loss function that represents the central bank’s ob-
jectives. Specifically, the central bank chooses the policy rate path that minimizes the expected
discounted value of the loss over a given horizon subject to the model’s behavioral equations.
Weights in the loss function capture the trade-off between output and inflation stabilization, and
the resulting model-based policy rate path is considered optimal for the specified weights and
model structure. However, the resulting path is only optimal for the specific model parameters
and is therefore sensitive to model uncertainty.
        Policy rules, linking the policy rate directly to a selection of macroeconomic variables, are
more widely used to describe monetary policy behavior. The canonical example is the Taylor
rule (Taylor, 1993), which describes how the nominal interest rate should respond systematically
to deviations in inflation from its target and output from potential. Although not necessarily
optimal, these rules offer a transparent description of monetary policy that can be easily com-
municated. Extensions and variations of the Taylor rule have since been developed to better fit
specific policy environments, incorporating elements such as exchange rate movements or policy
rate smoothing to reflect real world policy behavior (Nakamura et al., 2025).
        It is well established that effective monetary policy should be forward-looking (Svensson,
2010; Batini and Haldane, 1999) as monetary policy affects the economy with a lag and its
impact on GDP and inflation typically peaks after one to two years (Norges Bank, 2024). This
dimension can be incorporated into monetary policy rules. Forward-looking rules introduce
expectations explicitly, allowing the policy rate to respond to forecasts of inflation, output, and
other relevant variables rather than contemporaneous values. In addition to forecasts, such rules
can include indicators of inflation and output developments, which improves the responsiveness
of policy to underlying tendencies of monetary policy target variables.
        In forward-looking New Keynesian frameworks, optimal policy is determined by the expected
path of key economic variables (Galí, 2008; Clarida et al., 1999; Woodford, 2002). Therefore,
policy rules that incorporate forecasts and predictive indicators are likely better suited to esti-
mate optimal policy than those relying solely on current observations. Along with the policy
rate smoothing term, this implies that the policy rate responds more strongly to persistent,
medium-term developments and less to short-lived fluctuations and data noise, a response pat-
tern widely regarded as a desirable feature of sound monetary policy (Norges Bank monetary
policy strategy, 2025).
        To approximate NEMO’s optimal policy using an extended forward looking policy rule, we
introduce GEORG, "Ganske Enkel Optimal ReGel",1 which aims to offer a more transparent
complement to the current loss function approach used in NEMO. While the loss function implies
that the interest policy rate responds to all variables in the model, GEORG relies directly on a
selection of variables relevant to Norwegian monetary policy deliberation: inflation, output gap,
unit labor cost, the krone exchange rate, foreign interest rates and the money market premium.
The parameters associated with the variables in GEORG are obtained via using an impulse

in Norges Bank’s Monetary Policy Report in which changes to the contributions of exogenous
shocks are the basis for the decomposition analysis. Determining a relationship between forecast
revisions and exogenous shock decompositions requires deep knowledge of the model’s dynamics.
However, this alternative approach relates the decomposition analysis to forecast revisions,
which provides a clear mapping from observable forecast changes to the model-based policy
rate path.

market conditions and FX swap bases. µ̄ is the estimated steady-state money market premium.
       The loss function aims to minimize deviations in inflation from target and fluctuations in the
output gap, and to smooth policy rate changes over time. The selection of variables in GEORG
aims to incorporate macroeconomic indicators of inflation and output in order to implement a
forward-looking policy rule. As such, the right-hand side of GEORG should not be interpreted
as stabilization targets for the contemporaneous policy rate rt , but rather as providing a signal
of expected inflation and output, which rt aims to stabilize. Unit labor cost includes both
wage and productivity developments. Wage costs represent a large share of marginal costs for
domestic firms and can serve as an early indicator of future inflationary pressure. Higher wages,
all else equal, will also increase purchasing power and are likely to boost household demand.
An increase in productivity decreases the unit labor cost for a given wage level, mitigating
inflationary pressure. By explicitly responding to deviations in unit labor cost growth from its
steady-state level, monetary policy only responds to wage increases/decreases over and above
changes in productivity.
       In addition, the krone exchange rate contains useful information about the economic outlook,
as it affects inflation and activity through import prices and net exports with a considerable
transmission lag. Incorporating foreign policy rates provides information about international
economic developments and global monetary and financial developments. In addition, we di-
rectly include inflation and the output gap to capture all residual information regarding the
economic and inflation outlook not captured by the other indicators. Examples include Norges
Bank’s import price indicators (such as IPI and IPK, see for instance Brubakk et al. (2024) and
Norges Bank (2004)), price markups, and labor market developments. These variables are not
currently included in GEORG as they are not explicitly represented in NEMO. However, efforts
are ongoing to integrate them into Norges Bank’s forthcoming model revision.

3         Use of GEORG in practice
3.1         The model-based policy rate path
This section compares the policy rate path implied by NEMO with GEORG with the loss
function derived policy path. The objective is to assess how closely GEORG replicates the loss
function-based policy rate in NEMO. For the Monetary Policy Report (MPR) 4/2025, the policy
rate paths generated by GEORG and the loss function are nearly identical, and the resulting
developments in the output gap, inflation and the exchange rate are similar in the two versions
of NEMO.10 A similar pattern is observed for MPR 3/2025, MPR 2/2025 and MPR 1/2025.

3.2    Decomposition analysis
In the MPR, shock decompositions of the model’s policy rate paths are used to identify the
factors driving the change in the policy rate forecasts since the previous report. We can also
produce a shock decomposition when using NEMO with GEORG. In addition, GEORG al-
lows for a decomposition based on forecast revisions of the observable variables in the rule
(or changes in their steady-state values). This endogenous decomposition analysis translates
forecast revisions into changes in the policy rate contributions without relying on shock iden-
tification. Although this endogenous decomposition is reduced form, it is transparent and can
be replicated without detailed knowledge of the model’s structure.

      Even though the contributions are calculated mechanically using the rule’s coefficients, there
are some elements that could benefit from further explanation.
      The first is the absence of change in the residual (monetary policy shock), Zt . Zt appears
in equation (1) but does not appear in equation (4) above. The decomposition analysis aims to
illustrate how the model-based path has changed based on changes in the main drivers in the
model, and we therefore ignore the residual term. This is also the case for the decompositions
published in the Monetary Policy Report.
      It can also be useful to go into more detail on how a shift in r̄ would enter the decomposition.
An upward revision to the point estimate of r̄ in NEMO, all else equal, affects the policy path
in GEORG through two channels. First, it raises the level term as seen in equation (1) and
(4), which shifts the whole model-based path one-to-one. Second, it also enters the policy rate
smoothing term ωr (rt−1 − r̄t ), which partly offsets the level effect. As a result, since ωr > 0,
the policy rate from GEORG does not adjust one-to-one with the revision to r̄ in the short run.
Figure 3 illustrates these two effects and the resulting net contribution in the decomposition
due to a 25 basis points upward revision to r̄, all else equal.12

from changes in shock contributions. Overall, the aggregate change in the model-based policy
rate path from NEMO with GEORG is broadly similar to the loss function approach used in the
MPR. This is because the coefficients in GEORG are estimated to match the model dynamics of
the loss function version of NEMO. Nonetheless, the shock decomposition and the endogenous
decomposition based on NEMO with GEORG differ by definition as they are different objects.
In the shock decomposition, exchange rate bars typically represent risk premium shocks and
their full general equilibrium effects on the policy rate, including the delayed response of in-
flation. By contrast, in the GEORG (reduced form) decomposition, exchange rate bars show
only the role of revisions to the exchange rate forecasts and the delayed inflation responses
are reported separately under inflation. The same distinction applies to prices and wages: the
shock decomposition bundles price and wage markup shocks, while the GEORG decomposition
reports the isolated contribution of changes in price and wage forecasts.

4    Conclusion
This memo has documented GEORG, a fairly simple, transparent policy rule designed to ap-
proximate the loss function policy rate path from Norges Bank’s core model, NEMO. GEORG
provides a reduced form mapping of macroeconomic forecasts into an policy rate path. We show
that GEORG closely reproduces the dynamics of the loss function approach and can serve as
a practical and intuitive tool both for policy analysis and communication. The rule’s primary
strength lies in its transparency: it allows both internal and external users to trace how changes
in key forecasts affect the implied policy rate path in the model, providing a new endogenous
decomposition.
    Going forward, we expect GEORG to play a valuable role in Norges Bank’s analytical toolkit.
We will continue to develop GEORG going forward, particularly in combination with the devel-
opment of a new core model in Norges Bank. Improving the treatment of international impulses
is a key priority. Further efforts will also explore complementary rules as cross-checks under
alternative objectives. These may include rules that are robust across models or that implicitly
vary the weight on inflation stabilization relative to employment. Finally, we plan to examine
asymmetric reaction functions consistent with Norges Bank’s monetary policy strategy.

B        Estimation of coefficients
We estimate Ω = {ωr , ωπ , ωy , ωϕ , ωs , ωrf or , ωµ } such that the dynamic responses for GEORG
match those for the benchmark optimal policy (loss function) in NEMO (Kravik and Mimir,
2019). The impulse responses change as we vary the coefficients because economic agents are
forward-looking and internalize the central bank’s reaction function. When the parameters in
the rule change, the expected policy response changes as well, which in turn alters expectations
and the propagation of shocks. By searching over alternative parameter values, we identify the
set of coefficients that generates model dynamics most closely aligned with the optimal policy
in NEMO.
